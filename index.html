Health Care Availability

Despite the notion that health care should be a universal human right, there lies idiosyncrasies in many healthcare systems that discriminate against marginalized groups. This research is to bring awareness to possible disparities amongst different marginalized groups and their access and quality of healthcare. We aim to find why many healthcare systems disproportionately affect these groups, in hopes of finding solutions that can allow for more equal access and quality of healthcare.

Race, Ethnicity, and the Health Care System: Public Perceptions and Experiences

Click here to open this PDF in a new window


Despite the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and Medicare and Medicaid in 1965 there is still evidence that there lies unequal healthcare access for minority groups. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 “prohibited descriminations in institutions receiving federal funds.” The Medicare and Medicaid enactment of 1965 “reduced financial barriers to care for minority and low-income Americans.” However, there still lies disparities amongst marginalized groups in the United States healthcare system. The study above focuses on two of the United States largest racial and ethnic minority groups: African Americans and Latinos, and how they are disproportionately represented in the United States healthcare system. This issue is important because one in four Americans are classified as racial or ethnic minority according to the US Census. This research aims to answer four questions regarding the disparities in access to American healthcare. What extent is the public aware of racial differences in health care and health care access? Do whites, African Americans, and Latinos differ in their perceptions of how race and or racism affect the health care system? To what extent do individuals perceive that they have been treated unfairly because of their race or ethnic differences in health care and health care access? What do African Americans and Latinos perceive as major problems facing them in the healthcare system?

Gender Inequality In Health Care

In the health care industry, the availability has a major issue when it comes to gender. For example, In Bangladesh, clinics and hospitals are urban centered and access to money and power limit elderly rural women to access health care than elderly rural men. These elderly rural women are proven to suffer more elongated and rougher sickness than any other population group in Bangladesh because of reports of a high prevalence of morbidity and comorbidities and a low level of psychological well-being. With regard to access, the women experience a range of healthcare system factors and socioeconomic and cultural issues in their life in the places they live and work in that prevents them from accessing MHS. Many women are commonly diagnosed with fever, dysentery, asthma, bacterial infections, hearing loss and eye problems; these medically treatable diseases have an impact on chronic conditions for many women including hypertension (56.9%); arthritis (44.6%); diabetes (32.3%) and heart disease (20%). Even though these women are facing higher rates of diseases, more fatal diseases, and experience more difficulties with pychological well-being there still are being treated less than equal in regards to elderly men.

USDA Food and Nutrition Service

Did you know that the U.S. poverty rate increased to 11.2 percent in 2020; that is roughly 37.2 million people? Did you also know that low-income people obtain greater health risk than those with higher-income? It is understood that about 70 percent of the U.S. population who are uninsured are in poverty. Those who are uninsured and in poverty can not take preventative care and thus wait until an illness is severe to seek medical attention. This is often costly, and creates a vicious cycle for those in poverty. Those in poverty are also linked to inadequate health because their living conditions and stress rates are greater than those with a higher-income. Thus, people in poverty are usually more susceptible to the “material factors and psychological conditions that lead to poor health”(Poor and in Poor Health). Due to the fact that in the United States healthcare is not nationalized many health care insurance consumers face extremely high cost, inadequate coverage and limited health care options; this disproportionally affects Americans with a lower-income. There has been policy implemented to mitigate the strife of health care access for those in poverty. This includes the Affordable Care Act in which over 32 million Americans received affordable health care. The Affordable Care Act sought to provide coverage for those who are uninsured and also reduced the cost for those insured through reforming insurance market regulations. “The most promising aspect of the Affordable Care Act is its potential to reduce disparities in health, and in the long run to reduce disparities in health and earnings potential” —Barbara Wolfe. While policy is a step in the right direction in aiding those in poverty, there still lies bureaucratic inefficiency regarding healthcare in the United States. There are other possible options to increase the quality of health of those in low-income situations. Those who are faced with socio-economic factors often do not have the resources to practice healthy habits. Thus they take practice in habits they decrease the quality of their health, and leave them in the vicious cycle of the U.S. healthcare system. In an effort to raise awareness of health habits, we have provided the link to the USDA Food and Nutrition website(https://www.fns.usda.gov/). We hope this can provide resourceful information to improve the health of those with a lower-income, without having to rely on the United States healthcare system. The goal of the FNS is to “help end hunger, reduce diet-related disease, and accelerate health equity” (USDA Food and Nutrition Service).
